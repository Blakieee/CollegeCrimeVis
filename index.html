Overview

Campus safety is a top priority for institutions, students, and parents alike. By analyzing campus crime data from the past three years, we gain insights into patterns and priorities for creating safer learning environments.

Liquor and drug-related offenses dominate campus crime reports, making up the majority of incidents. This highlights the need for targeted interventions, such as substance abuse education and enforcement of campus policies.

Yearly Change in Crime Types

While major crime types dominate the overall trends, smaller categories have shown notable shifts. We visualize the year-over-year differences in crime totals, revealing both alarming and promising trends.

Among the most striking observations is the sharp rise in Motor Vehicle Theft, which tops the chart with an increase of over 4,000 cases between 2021 and 2022. This upward trajectory calls for immediate attention to parking lot security and theft deterrence.

Stalking has also seen a significant uptick, requiring stronger protections for victims and better awareness campaigns. Meanwhile, categories like Dating Violence and Burglary show noticeable changes, pointing to opportunities for intervention.

Encouragingly, some crimes, such as Incest, have remained at a low baseline. Rape and Fondling have shown declining patterns, offering a glimmer of hope that education, awareness campaigns, and institutional policies are making an impact.

Understanding campus crime trends is just the beginning of addressing safety challenges in educational institutions.

What shapes campus crime patterns?

Key influences include state crime rates, school control and rankings, political climates, and local economic conditions. Examining these factors reveals how broader contexts intersect with campus safety, offering opportunities for targeted solutions.

Campus Crime & State Crime

While liquor is the top crime type across campuses, it accounts for only a very limited portion of state crimes. On the other hand, the second-highest crime type on campus, Drug-related offenses, is also the dominant offense in state crime. Compared to the top campus crimes, violent crime appears to take up a significant portion.

State vs. Campus Crime

Now, if we compare state crimes and campus crimes side-by-side, we observe that while Texas and California are the two states with the most state crimes reported, the highest campus crime concentration actually occurs in Colorado and Indiana, followed by South Carolina. We would assume that the density of state crime aligns with that of the campus crime, which brings us to consider the issue of data humanism.

Crime Rate Distribution by University Type

Based on the chart data, crime rates vary more among private universities than public ones. The spread of crime rates (interquartile range) is wider for private universities (0.41%) compared to public universities (0.28%).

Also, the median crime rate is higher for private universities (0.33%) than for public universities (0.22%). Some private universities have very high crime rates exceeding 1.2% , while public universities have fewer and less severe extreme cases.

QS 2021 Rankings and Crime Rates

Blue dots represent university rankings decreasing from left to right, and colored lines show crime rates — grey indicate lower rates, while red signify higher rates.

Among the top 70 QS-ranked universities, Stanford, the University of Michigan, and NC State have notably higher crime rates (red lines). Johns Hopkins, Dartmouth, and Notre Dame also have moderately high rates. Universities ranked beyond 70 generally display lower and more uniform crime rates (mostlygrey lines), suggesting better campus safety.

Political Climate

The common belief that liberal areas inherently have higher crime rates often extends to campuses. However, by comparing campus crime rates with state political leanings (based on the 2020 election results), we uncover trends that challenge assumptions and highlight regional disparities.

The color shows the clear partisan divide of states (Democratic-leaning and Republican-leaning), while the cartogram states are resized based on their average campus crime rates, revealing a shift in proportions.

Republican-leaning states account for 56.97% of the total adjusted area, compared to 43.03% for Democratic-leaning states. This suggests that campuses in Republican-dominated states experience proportionally higher average crime rates, challenging the common narrative associating liberal areas with higher crime.

This disproportionate representation in the cartogram also highlights a significant contribution from certain Republican-leaning states, most notably Texas.

As the largest outlier, Texas alone drives much of this imbalance, with an average campus crime rate of 19.83%—a figure that far exceeds the rates observed in both Republican and Democratic-leaning states, which generally remain below 8.5%.

Such a stark contrast invites deeper scrutiny into the unique factors at play in Texas, including its extensive campus networks, the influence of statewide policies, and potential disparities in crime reporting practices.

Crime Rate VS Unemployment Rate

Counties with higher employment often show elevated crime rates: Green points, representing higher employment levels, cluster in areas with crime rates reaching 10%-15%. These counties are likely urban areas with significant economic activity, which may create opportunities for both legitimate work and increased crime due to population density and resource competition.

A negative correlation exists between unemployment and crime rates: The regression line highlights that as unemployment increases, crime rates tend to decrease. This may reflect the nature of rural or suburban areas, where higher unemployment is often paired with lower population density, reducing the likelihood of crimes driven by social and economic pressures.

Undergraduate population impacts crime and unemployment patterns: Counties with larger undergraduate populations (represented by bigger circles) tend to have mid-level unemployment rates (4%-8%) and varying crime rates. This suggests that the presence of higher education institutions creates a stabilizing effect on both employment opportunities and social behaviors.

High-unemployment areas often have lower crime rates: Sparse points above 10% unemployment generally show crime rates below 5%, representing rural or economically stagnant regions. These areas, despite economic challenges, may benefit from stronger community cohesion or lower population density, limiting crime opportunities.

Conclusion

In analyzing the relationship between campus crime and various socioeconomic and political factors, we identified several intriguing patterns. Some findings, such as the correlation between higher undergraduate populations and relatively stable socioeconomic conditions, suggest that education and institutional resources contribute to campus safety. Additionally, the data revealed surprising insights, such as higher campus crime rates in some Republican-leaning states, challenging conventional assumptions about the influence of political leanings on safety. These observations underscore the importance of considering diverse factors—such as local economic conditions, state policies, and demographic distributions—when assessing campus crime.

However, it is important to acknowledge that not all data presented strong or consistent trends as initially expected. For instance, while some correlations, like the inverse relationship between crime rates and unemployment, appeared in certain regions, they were less pronounced in others, highlighting significant variability. This suggests that campus crime is influenced by complex, multifaceted factors that resist simple explanations. Future research should incorporate more granular data and explore additional variables, such as cultural dynamics, crime reporting practices, and community engagement, to provide a more comprehensive understanding of campus safety across the United States.
